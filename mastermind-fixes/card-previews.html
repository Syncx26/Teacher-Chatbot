Concept

The Attention Mechanism

Instead of reading a sentence word-by-word, transformers look at every word simultaneously and decide which ones are most relevant to each other. This "attention score" lets the model understand context across the entire input at once.

Think of it like…

A hiring manager scanning all 200 CVs at once and assigning a "relevance score" to each, rather than reading them one by one.

Attention Score

A number between 0 and 1 that measures how much one token should influence the meaning of another.

Exercise

A transformer processes a 10-word sentence. How many attention comparisons does the model make?

Answer

100 comparisons (10 × 10). Every word attends to every other word, including itself — that's n² comparisons for n tokens.

This quadratic cost is why long contexts are expensive. Modern architectures like Flash Attention reduce this to O(n).

Real Story

The Night Attention Was Invented

In 2017, eight Google researchers sat in a conference room and wrote the sentence "Attention is all you need" as a joke title. They nearly changed it before publishing. Within three years, that mechanism ended the dominance of recurrent networks and set off the LLM era — GPT, BERT, Gemini, and Claude all trace their lineage to that document. The joke title became the defining phrase of modern AI.

Source: "Attention Is All You Need", Vaswani et al., NeurIPS 2017

Hot Take

LLMs Don't Understand Language

A provocative but defensible claim: transformers don't understand meaning — they model the statistical co-occurrence of tokens. Ask GPT-4 to count the letters in "strawberry" and it fails. Yet it writes poetry that moves people. The debate over whether this constitutes "understanding" may matter less than the practical question: does it matter for your use case?
